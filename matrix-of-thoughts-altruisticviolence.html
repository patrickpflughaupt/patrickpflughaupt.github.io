Altruistic Violence



For as long as I can remember, reading books of any kind was at the very bottom of my list of interests. Mariana Trench sort of bottom of list. Why, I do not know, but things have changed this summer. I picked up Unnatural Causes by Dr. Richard Shepherd and next thing you know, I devoured seven additional medical biographies and watched Netflix’s Lenox Hill, all within two months. A level of fascination for medicine and specialist surgeons I could not translate into words. It always felt like a well choreographed performance when experiencing the gruesome but immaculate detailed processes in operating theatres, and yet I flinched when this exact process was shown on Lenox Hill. I had to wait for Do No Harm by Dr. Henry Marsh to finally realise that I, too, found its controlled and altruistic violence deeply appealing. Although, at first, I was pulled back by this remark - altruism? Yes, but violence? An oxymoron, surely? But with a bit of contemplation, I came to terms with this strange, but perfectly efficient juxtaposition. 

Surgery is, no doubt, a dangerous sport. Deeply complicated procedures are carried out in an attempt to prolong human life but with it comes a burden of responsibility, and thus, risk. To master a skill, you need a never ending amount of practice, trial and error, mistakes to learn from and risks to take to innovate medical practices. And while lives are lost in the process of this, it is done for the greater good because one day, when it is my turn to be practiced upon, I sincerely hope that the mastery of their skill comforts me with the anaesthetic. Equally, if I become part of their statistics, I know that I contributed to the knowledge of medical practices. Altruistic violence. 

To me, I was most drawn towards the practice of neurosurgery and the understanding of our brain. Not because of any interest in operating on this delicate piece, nor do I have any motivation for this, but it feels like the Everest of medicine. It defines us, allows us to have hopes, ambitions and experience emotions - love, anger, hate, joy, disgust, fear, grief. It allows you to read and understand what I have translated from mere thoughts into words. The epitome of art and science. We simply function because of a conglomeration of, as Dr. Henry put it, electrochemical chatter of our nerve cells. However, while we have one hundred billion nerve cells in our brains, we, frankly, don’t know too much about it. Does each one of the nerve cells have a fragment of consciousness within it? How many of these do we require to be conscious or to feel pain? Or does consciousness and thought reside in the electrochemical impulses that join these billions of cells together? Questions raised in the book but we simply do not have the answer to, yet. 

I really hope that generations from now, we will understand this through perpetual research endeavours and altruistic violence.
