Unforgotten



A secret kept hidden away for half a century and three years to untangle, I am grateful to have read The Tattooist of Auschwitz. It’s another reminder of the atrocious and barbaric conditions that innocent people like Ludwig "Lale" Sokolov had to endure in concentration camps. 

Lale was put to work in a comparatively privileged position as the “Tätowierer” - the tattooist - to apply permanent ink onto the wrists of arriving prisoners. With this role, he received extra rations, slept in a single room and had “free time” after finishing his work. Any extra ration he received, he would smuggle it to neighbouring blocks of prisoners where he would distribute it and try to help keep the morale up. 

When Lale met a girl called Gita, it was love at first sight. They would make every effort possible to meet in the shadows of SS guards, even if just for a brief period, with the hope of one day living a life together outside of this camp, forever.

It was beautiful and wholesome to experience a story about love that kept them together in a place where paths were destined to be different. A place where its destination was the very destruction of love.

To save one is to save the world.
